\begin{abstract}
Automated detection of Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) from peripheral blood smear microscopy is constrained in practice by the computational demands of high-performing models and by evaluation protocols that permit patient-level data leakage, producing inflated performance estimates unsuitable for clinical translation. This paper presents a fully self-contained screening pipeline that combines K-Means and watershed-based cell localisation, Segment Anything Model (SAM) boundary refinement, and INT8-quantized lightweight convolutional classifiers (MobileNetV3-Large and EfficientNet-B0) with 4-way test-time augmentation and Youden's J threshold calibration, deployed locally without cloud connectivity and accompanied by an automated clinical report generated by a locally-hosted language model. Evaluated under a strict 3-fold subject-disjoint cross-validation protocol across 101 patients from the C-NMC 2019 cohort --- a design that prevents the patient-level staining artifact leakage common in prior work --- MobileNetV3-Large achieves a mean AUC of $0.962 \pm 0.006$, ALL-class sensitivity of $92.1\% \pm 1.5\%$, and per-cell inference latency of 695\,ms on local consumer hardware. The system is designed as an assistive pre-screening tool to reduce pathologist workload in low-resource settings and does not replace expert morphological diagnosis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{A}{cute} Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) is the most prevalent pediatric malignancy, characterized by the unabated proliferation of immature lymphoid cells \cite{b_bray2018}. The accepted clinical standard for morphological screening requires the manual inspection of stained peripheral blood smears via light microscopy by trained hematopathologists. While diagnostically crucial, this protocol is labor-intensive, time-consuming, and subject to inter-observer variability. Consequently, it creates a severe screening bottleneck in low-resource and point-of-care settings where specialized hematological expertise is scarce.

Existing deep learning methods for ALL detection report high accuracy figures but routinely use image-level random splits that permit patient-specific staining signatures to appear in both training and validation partitions \cite{b3, b4, b7, b10}. This leakage inflates reported metrics by an estimated 10--15 percentage points relative to independent subject-disjoint evaluation. Furthermore, the highest-performing models rely on recurrent or transformer-based architectures with parameter counts and inference latencies incompatible with local execution on restricted hardware.

We design a pipeline that addresses both limitations simultaneously: it enforces a subject-disjoint patient-level split and builds the entire evaluation around hardware-executable lightweight convolutional backbones. This conservative methodology produces reproducible performance estimates grounded in the reality of clinical deployment constraints.

The specific contributions of this work are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A 3-fold subject-disjoint cross-validation protocol over an expanded 101-patient C-NMC cohort that prevents patient-level data leakage, providing conservative but reproducible performance estimates.
\item A hybrid cell segmentation pipeline combining L*a*b* K-Means clustering, Euclidean Distance Transform watershed separation, and SAM ViT-Base prompt-based refinement for standardised cell crop extraction.
\item A progressive backbone unfreezing training strategy with a corrected mild focal loss ($\gamma=0.5$, $\alpha=[0.75, 0.25]$) that remains an informative training signal for the full 150-epoch schedule.
\item A complete end-to-end inference application with INT8 TFLite quantization, 4-way TTA, per-fold Youden's J threshold calibration, and locally-hosted Phi-3 clinical report generation, benchmarked on consumer hardware without cloud connectivity.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the dataset and preprocessing protocol; Section~\ref{sec:method} details the training pipeline and inference application; Section~\ref{sec:results} presents quantitative classification and latency results with comparison to prior work; Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses deployment constraints and limitations; Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Automated ALL Detection}
Early computational methods for leukemia detection relied on hand-crafted features extracted from cell geometry and texture. Recent efforts shifted toward deep convolutional architectures applied directly to cell images. Shafique and Tehsin \cite{b3} and Thanh et al. \cite{b4} applied ImageNet-pretrained networks to achieve high nominal classification rates. More recently, Mohammed et al. \cite{b7} reported accuracies above 95\% using an ensemble of CNN and recurrent structures, while Rajaraman et al. \cite{b10} achieved 98\% accuracy using Falcon optimization applied to deep autoencoders. The explicit gap in this literature is the evaluation protocol: Mohammed et al. \cite{b7} and Rajaraman et al. \cite{b10} both report accuracy above 95\% using image-level splits; neither reports results under a subject-disjoint protocol. Image-level splits on cell image datasets permit the same patient's cells --- sharing identical staining conditions and morphological artifacts --- to populate both training and validation sets, producing optimistic estimates that do not generalise to new patients. Subject-disjoint evaluation on the same dataset typically yields accuracy figures 10--15 percentage points lower than image-level evaluation, not because the model is weaker but because the evaluation is more honest.

\subsection{Lightweight CNN Architectures for Medical Imaging}
MobileNetV3 \cite{b_howard2019}, building upon the inverted residuals of MobileNetV2 \cite{b_mobilenet_v2}, and EfficientNet \cite{b12} represent the primary lightweight backbone families used in resource-constrained medical imaging. Raghu et al. \cite{b_raghu2019} showed that ImageNet-pretrained models transfer effectively to medical tasks even with limited domain data. Both families achieve competitive accuracy at a fraction of the parameter count of VGG or ResNet baselines \cite{b2}. Progressive unfreezing is a standard transfer learning technique for adapting pretrained backbones to medical domains \cite{b1}. The head layers are trained first; deeper backbone stages are unlocked in subsequent phases to avoid catastrophic forgetting. 

\subsection{Edge Deployment of Medical AI}
Post-training INT8 quantization reduces network precision from 32-bit floating point to 8-bit integers, cutting model size by approximately 4$\times$ and enabling inference on CPUs without GPU hardware \cite{b_quant_survey, b_jacob2018}. Qayyum et al. \cite{b_qayyum2020} and Venugopal et al. \cite{b_venugopal2022} demonstrated compressed medical imaging models deployed on edge devices, but both focused on model compression in isolation without integrating a complete segmentation-to-report pipeline. This work addresses the full clinical pipeline from raw microscopy ingestion through to clinical text generation. No prior work on ALL detection has jointly addressed subject-disjoint evaluation, edge-executable inference, and end-to-end clinical reporting in a single system.

\section{Dataset and Preprocessing}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{C-NMC 2019 Dataset}
The C-NMC 2019 dataset \cite{b5} consists of segmented mononuclear cell images collected from peripheral blood smears of ALL patients and healthy controls. Original images are standard sequence RGB crops provided at variable resolutions. The total dataset employed in this work consists of 12,528 distinct cell crops (8,491 ALL crops and 4,037 HEM crops) extracted from 101 clinically distinct patients. The dataset was originally released with a training partition of 73 patients and a preliminary evaluation partition of 28 patients; this work merges both partitions into a single pool to maximise the number of distinct patients available for cross-validation evaluation. Throughout this work, ALL cells constitute the positive class; sensitivity therefore denotes the true positive rate for leukemic cell detection and specificity denotes the true negative rate for normal cell identification.

\subsection{Subject-Disjoint Cross-Validation Protocol}
Image-level random splits are insufficient for this dataset: cells from the same patient share staining conditions, scanner characteristics, and morphological artifacts; allowing the same patient to appear in both training and validation sets introduces a form of data leakage that inflates all reported metrics. This work enforces patient-level exclusivity: no patient's cells appear in both the training and validation sets of any fold. The exact patient counts track tightly per fold (e.g., Fold 1 uses 63 patients in training and 38 in validation). Leakage was verified programmatically by checking that the set of patient identifiers in training and validation partitions is completely disjoint for every fold.

\subsection{Class Imbalance}
The dataset demonstrates a distinct class imbalance, maintaining a ratio of 8,491 ALL cells against 4,037 HEM cells. Two mechanisms address this imbalance: a WeightedRandomSampler that resamples each training epoch to produce a balanced 50/50 class distribution, and per-class weights in the loss function that apply additional gradient pressure. Training accuracy is therefore measured on a synthetic balanced distribution and is not directly comparable to validation accuracy, which is measured on the natural distribution; all performance comparisons use validation metrics only.

\subsection{Augmentation}
Training images undergo the following augmentations applied in sequence: resize to $352 \times 352$ followed by random crop to $320 \times 320$; random horizontal flip ($p=0.5$); random vertical flip ($p=0.5$); random 90-degree rotation ($p=0.5$); random affine transform with shear range $[-10^{\circ}, 10^{\circ}]$ and scale range $[0.9, 1.1]$ ($p=0.5$); elastic deformation with $\alpha=1$, $\sigma=10$ ($p=0.3$); color jitter with brightness, contrast, and saturation perturbation of $\pm 0.1$ and hue perturbation of $\pm 0.02$ ($p=0.5$), simulating H\&E staining variation across scanners; Gaussian blur with kernel size sampled from $\{3, 5, 7\}$ ($p=0.2$); CoarseDropout with 8 rectangular occlusion patches of size $32 \times 32$ ($p=0.2$). All images are normalised using ImageNet channel statistics ($\mu=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406]$, $\sigma=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]$) prior to backbone ingestion, consistent with ImageNet pre-training conditions.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Model Training Pipeline}
Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture} illustrates the complete training pipeline.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/architecture.png}}
\caption{Training pipeline. The diagram shows the flow from C-NMC data ingestion and subject-disjoint fold assignment through augmentation, progressive backbone unfreezing across four phases, AdamW/cosine optimisation, and post-training Youden's J threshold calibration to ONNX and TFLite export.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Network Architecture}
Each model consists of a pre-trained backbone (MobileNetV3-Large \cite{b_howard2019} or EfficientNet-B0 \cite{b12}) with its original classification head removed, feeding into a custom classification head. The head architecture is exactly: Batch Normalisation $\rightarrow$ Dropout($p=0.4$) $\rightarrow$ Linear($d_{\mathrm{feat}} \rightarrow 512$) $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ Dropout($p=0.3$) $\rightarrow$ Linear($512 \rightarrow 2$), where $d_{\mathrm{feat}}$ is the backbone's output feature dimension (960 for MobileNetV3-Large, 1280 for EfficientNet-B0). The total parameter count for the MobileNetV3-Large variant is 4.86 million. Input resolution is fixed at $320 \times 320$.

\subsubsection{Progressive Backbone Unfreezing}
Training proceeds in four distinct phases:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Phase 0 (epochs 1--10):} backbone frozen, head only.
\item \textbf{Phase 1 (epochs 11--20):} last two backbone stages unfrozen at $0.5 \times lr_{\mathrm{backbone}}$.
\item \textbf{Phase 1.5 (epochs 21--30):} last four backbone stages unfrozen at $0.75 \times lr_{\mathrm{backbone}}$.
\item \textbf{Phase 2 (epochs 31+):} full backbone unfrozen at $lr_{\mathrm{backbone}}$.
\end{itemize}
Backbone stages are identified by named parameter prefixes rather than by position in the module hierarchy, to ensure correct stage selection across backbone architectures. The cosine annealing scheduler resets at each phase transition so that each phase begins its own decay from the new learning rate.

\subsubsection{Loss Function}
The system applies a mild focal loss ($\gamma=0.5$) combined with label smoothing ($\epsilon=0.05$). At standard settings ($\gamma=2.0$), once the model classifies the majority of training samples with high confidence, the focal weights reduce each sample's gradient contribution to near zero, causing the reported loss to continue decreasing while validation AUC has plateaued --- the loss becomes a misleading signal. At $\gamma=0.5$, a sample classified at 90\% confidence still contributes approximately 32\% of the gradient of a chance-level sample, keeping the loss informative for the full training schedule. An asymmetric priority vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}=[0.75, 0.25]$ is applied as a multiplicative weight on top of the normalised inverse-frequency class weights derived from the training partition; the two are combined element-wise and passed as the per-class weight argument to the cross-entropy base loss. This imposes an additional gradient penalty on ALL misclassification beyond the frequency correction already provided by class-balanced sampling, reflecting the clinical asymmetry between a missed cancer diagnosis and a false alarm on a healthy cell.

\subsubsection{Optimisation Schedule}
Training employs the AdamW optimiser \cite{b_adamw} with an initial backbone learning rate of $10^{-5}$, head learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-4}$, and weight decay of $10^{-4}$. Linear warmup dictates the first 10 epochs ramping from $0.5\times$ to the target rates. Cosine annealing extends from epoch 11 to the end of training with minimum learning rate parameter $\eta_{\mathrm{min}} = 10^{-7}$ \cite{b_cosine}. Gradient clipping operates at $\|\nabla\|_2 = 1.0$. Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) accelerates all GPU training runs.

\subsubsection{Post-Training Threshold Calibration}
The best checkpoint per fold is selected according to peak validation AUC. The decision threshold is then swept over $\theta \in [0.35, 0.75]$ at intervals of $0.05$ on the validation set. The threshold maximising Youden's J statistic ($J = \mathrm{Sensitivity} + \mathrm{Specificity} - 1$) \cite{b_youden} is recorded as $\theta_{\mathrm{opt}}$ for that fold. For the deployed application, a separate conservative threshold value ($\tau = 0.85$) is additionally applied to minimise alerts on ambiguous cases.

\subsubsection{Model Export}
Each best checkpoint is exported to ONNX format (opset 13, dynamic batch axis). The ONNX graph is then converted to TFLite with INT8 post-training quantization \cite{b_jacob2018}, reducing model size and enabling inference on hardware without a GPU.

\subsection{End-to-End Inference Application}
Fig.~\ref{fig:demo_pipeline} illustrates the complete inference pipeline from raw microscopy image to structured clinical report.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/demo_pipeline.png}}
\caption{End-to-end inference pipeline. Stage~1: L*a*b* K-Means and watershed cell localisation. Stage~2: SAM ViT-Base boundary refinement. Stage~3: background suppression and $320 \times 320$ crop standardisation. Stage~4: 4-way TTA classification using the INT8 TFLite model. Stage~5: Phi-3 clinical report generation on the local AI Dashboard.}
\label{fig:demo_pipeline}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Cell Localisation}
Cell localisation begins with K-Means clustering ($K=3$) in the a*b* chrominance channels of L*a*b* colour space to separate leukocyte nuclei from erythrocytes \cite{b_kmeans_lab}. A Euclidean Distance Transform followed by marker-controlled watershed segmentation splits touching cells \cite{b_watershed}. The output is one centroid coordinate per candidate cell.

\subsubsection{SAM Boundary Refinement}
Each centroid is used as a single point prompt for SAM ViT-Base \cite{b_sam}, which generates a pixel-accurate cell boundary mask. SAM ViT-Base requires GPU acceleration for practical throughput; on CPU-only hardware, the classical watershed boundary serves as a fallback.

\subsubsection{Crop Standardisation}
The SAM mask suppresses background pixels surrounding the cell. A centred bounding box crop is resized to $320 \times 320$ and normalised with ImageNet channel statistics. The output is one standardised $320 \times 320$ tensor per detected cell.

\subsubsection{TTA Classification}
The standardised crop is passed through the INT8 TFLite model four times: original orientation, horizontal flip, vertical flip, and 180-degree rotation \cite{b_tta}. The four softmax output vectors are averaged element-wise to produce the final class probability estimate. The TTA-averaged $P(\mathrm{ALL})$ is compared against $\tau$ (deployment) or $\theta_{\mathrm{opt}}$ (evaluation).

\subsubsection{Clinical Report Generation}
Flagged cells and confidence scores are passed to a locally-hosted Phi-3 language model (Ollama). The model generates a structured clinical summary listing per-cell results, confidence distributions, and a pathologist review recommendation. Results are displayed on a local AI Dashboard. All outputs are assistive flags for human review and do not constitute a clinical diagnosis.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Classification Performance}
MobileNetV3-Large achieves a mean AUC of $0.962 \pm 0.006$ and ALL-class sensitivity of $92.1\% \pm 1.5\%$ under subject-disjoint evaluation (Table~\ref{tab:metrics}). 4-way TTA was applied during validation; the expected per-sample variance reduction from multi-orientation averaging is documented in \cite{b_tta}, and its contribution to the reported AUC figures is not separately ablated in this work. EfficientNet-B0 achieves a similar AUC of $0.963 \pm 0.004$ with ALL sensitivity of $90.7\% \pm 3.8\%$ and HEM specificity of $89.0\% \pm 3.6\%$. Fig.~\ref{fig:loss} shows that training and validation loss converge by epoch 40 and remain stable through the final epoch. Fig.~\ref{fig:auc} confirms that mean validation AUC plateaus near 0.96 after the Phase~2 transition at epoch 31. Fig.~\ref{fig:metrics_all} shows that ALL sensitivity stabilises above 85\% from epoch 50 onward while HEM specificity stabilises near 90\%.

\subsection{Local Inference Latency}
The MobileNetV3-Large single-fold pipeline processes one cell crop in 695\,ms on average, with the full field-of-view pipeline completing in 10.37\,s on local consumer hardware (Table~\ref{tab:edgebench}). The 3-model ensemble increases per-cell latency to 2,038\,ms and total pipeline time to 26.33\,s. These latencies are compatible with a point-of-care triage workflow that does not require real-time throughput.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Subject-Disjoint Evaluation and Metric Interpretation}
The 81--84\% accuracy figures reported here are lower than the 95--97\% figures in prior work on the same dataset \cite{b7, b10}. The gap is methodological, not architectural: image-level random splits allow patient-specific staining signatures to leak across partitions, inflating accuracy by 10--15 percentage points. Subject-disjoint evaluation is the only protocol that estimates how the model will perform on patients not seen during training.

\subsection{Deployment Constraints}
SAM ViT-Base inference on CPU is substantially slower than the downstream INT8 classifier; on the benchmark hardware, SAM mask generation accounts for the majority of total pipeline latency. For CPU-only deployment, the classical watershed boundary provides an acceptable segmentation fallback that eliminates the SAM dependency entirely.

\subsection{Clinical Scope and Safety}
This pipeline is a pre-screening triage tool, not a diagnostic device. The threshold $\tau=0.85$ was chosen to minimise ALL false negatives at the cost of increased false alarm rate on healthy slides, which is the correct trade-off for a first-line screening instrument. All outputs require verification by a qualified haematopathologist before clinical action.

\subsection{Limitations}
Four limitations apply. (1) All evaluations use a single 101-patient dataset from one institution; no external validation cohort has been tested, leaving domain shift \cite{b6} unaddressed. (2) No prospective clinical trial has been conducted. (3) SAM ViT-Base inference speed precludes real-time GPU-free deployment without the watershed fallback. (4) The Phi-3 report generator has not been evaluated for clinical accuracy or regulatory compliance.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper presents a self-contained pipeline for assistive ALL detection, validated under a 3-fold subject-disjoint cross-validation protocol that prevents patient-level data leakage and produces reproducible performance estimates. MobileNetV3-Large achieves a mean AUC of $0.962 \pm 0.006$ with ALL-class sensitivity of $92.1\% \pm 1.5\%$ under this protocol; EfficientNet-B0 achieves AUC $0.963 \pm 0.004$ with ALL-class sensitivity of $90.7\% \pm 3.8\%$. INT8 quantization reduces MobileNetV3-Large to an edge-executable format, achieving a mean per-cell inference latency of 695\,ms on consumer hardware without GPU acceleration.

The system is intended for use in resource-limited clinical laboratories as an assistive pre-screening tool to prioritise slides for pathologist review.

Future work should address three directions: (1) external validation on an independent multi-institution cohort to assess generalisation across laboratory conditions; (2) replacement of SAM ViT-Base with a lightweight segmentation model to enable full sub-second CPU deployment; and (3) a prospective clinical trial under an ethics protocol to establish the system's utility as a pathologist decision-support tool.
